🧠 Search Strategy

Boolean Operators

AND — narrows results; both terms must be present (e.g. diabetes AND exercise).
OR — broadens results; either term can be present (e.g. heart attack OR myocardial infarction).
NOT — excludes a term (use sparingly — can remove relevant records).

MeSH Terms (Medical Subject Headings)

PubMed indexes every article using a controlled vocabulary called MeSH. Using [MeSH Terms] after a term retrieves all articles tagged with that concept, even if different words are used. MeSH terms explode by default — meaning they also search narrower sub-terms.

Field Tags

[Title/Abstract] — searches only the title and abstract fields.
[MeSH Terms] — searches the controlled MeSH vocabulary.
[Author] — searches by author name.

Phrase Searching & Truncation

Wrap phrases in double quotes: "heart failure".
Use * for truncation: therap* matches therapy, therapies, therapeutic.

Why use a structured strategy?

A well-documented search strategy is reproducible, defensible, and required when publishing a systematic review. It ensures you retrieve all relevant literature while keeping irrelevant results manageable.

What is PRISMA?

PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) is an evidence-based set of items for reporting in systematic reviews and meta-analyses. The 2020 update added more detail on the search and screening process. Most health and social science journals require PRISMA compliance when submitting a systematic review.

The Four Phases

  • Identification — Records retrieved from databases and other sources before any screening.
  • Screening — Records assessed on title and abstract against your inclusion/exclusion criteria. Most records are removed here.
  • Eligibility — Remaining records assessed in full text (full-text screening). This app currently supports title/abstract screening only.
  • Included — Studies that meet all criteria and are included in the synthesis or review.

Why Report Duplicates?

The same paper often appears in multiple databases. Recording how many duplicates were removed shows your search was comprehensive (you searched multiple sources) and your deduplication was systematic.

How to Use This in Your Paper

Copy the narrative above into your Methods section under "Search strategy and study selection". Include the flowchart as a figure. Report the date(s) the databases were searched, the search strings used, and who performed the screening.
